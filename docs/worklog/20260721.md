# 21 July 2026

## Why the external drive felt slow

Plugged an old external hard drive into the laptop and it felt sluggish.
Wanted to know whether the drive itself was failing or the connection was
just slow, before spending money on anything.

**It was the adapter, not the drive.** The machine's USB tree showed the
drive negotiating at the older, slower USB speed even though the laptop has a
faster port sitting unused. So the drive was being throttled to roughly a
quarter of what it could manage — and for a spinning drive like this one, that
is a real bottleneck, not a paper one.

**Checked the drive's health before blaming it, and it came out clean.** The
drive's own self-diagnosis reported no bad sectors, nothing pending, nothing
remapped — the handful of numbers that actually predict a failing disk were
all zero. A short self-test the drive ran on itself finished without error.
The drive is barely used: a few months of actual running time in total.

The interesting part was in the error log. The *old* errors were genuine
read problems on the platters, but they were years ago and had since healed.
The *recent* errors were a different kind entirely — commands being cut off
mid-flight — which is the signature of a flaky connection rather than a dying
disk. The adapter was also too primitive to pass the drive's health questions
through properly, which was another tell.

**Decided to replace the adapter, not the drive.** Looked at a cheap enclosure
online. It ticked the one box that matters — it speaks the faster protocol —
though it did not name the exact chip inside. Concluded that for a slow drive
like this the unnamed chip barely matters: any working modern adapter clears
the drive's real speed easily, and a dud would announce itself on the first
test rather than fail quietly. Named a couple of better-known alternatives as
safer bets at a similar price, but either way the plan is the same.

Nothing to do with the site — just a bit of hardware detective work that ended
with a clear answer: keep the drive, bin the adapter.
