# 21 July 2026

## Why the external drive felt slow

Plugged an old external hard drive into the laptop and it felt sluggish.
Wanted to know whether the drive itself was failing or the connection was
just slow, before spending money on anything.

**It was the adapter, not the drive.** The machine's USB tree showed the
drive negotiating at the older, slower USB speed even though the laptop has a
faster port sitting unused. So the drive was being throttled to roughly a
quarter of what it could manage — and for a spinning drive like this one, that
is a real bottleneck, not a paper one.

**Checked the drive's health before blaming it, and it came out clean.** The
drive's own self-diagnosis reported no bad sectors, nothing pending, nothing
remapped — the handful of numbers that actually predict a failing disk were
all zero. A short self-test the drive ran on itself finished without error.
The drive is barely used: a few months of actual running time in total.

The interesting part was in the error log. The *old* errors were genuine
read problems on the platters, but they were years ago and had since healed.
The *recent* errors were a different kind entirely — commands being cut off
mid-flight — which is the signature of a flaky connection rather than a dying
disk. The adapter was also too primitive to pass the drive's health questions
through properly, which was another tell.

**Decided to replace the adapter, not the drive.** Looked at a cheap enclosure
online. It ticked the one box that matters — it speaks the faster protocol —
though it did not name the exact chip inside. Concluded that for a slow drive
like this the unnamed chip barely matters: any working modern adapter clears
the drive's real speed easily, and a dud would announce itself on the first
test rather than fail quietly. Named a couple of better-known alternatives as
safer bets at a similar price, but either way the plan is the same.

Nothing to do with the site — just a bit of hardware detective work that ended
with a clear answer: keep the drive, bin the adapter.

## A second, much older drive — and why its health check was harder

Swapped in another drive to check the same way: an old 80 GB one that holds the
television's media. Two things made it a different problem.

First, its health could not be read at all. The little adapter refused to pass
the drive's self-diagnosis questions through — and this was an even older *kind*
of drive, from before the current connector standard, sitting behind a bridge
that simply does not forward those questions. Tried every polite way to ask;
none worked. So the usual shortcut was off the table.

The fallback was to read the entire drive from end to end and watch for any
part that would not come back. That is the honest test anyway — if every spot
on the surface can be read, the surface is sound. Nearly an hour later it had
read all 80 GB with not a single error. The drive is healthy; the 66 GB of
television content on it is intact. No recovery needed.

Worth remembering: **when a drive won't report its own health, read the whole
surface instead.** It is slower, but it answers the only question that matters —
can everything still be read?

## The thing I nearly got wrong about speed

I almost assumed a new adapter would speed this old drive up too. The numbers
said otherwise. It read at about 23 units of speed, while even the old slow
connection can carry roughly 40 — so the connection was never the bottleneck.
The drive itself is just slow, being small and old. A faster adapter cannot
make a drive hand over data faster than it can physically read it.

That is the opposite of the first drive, where the connection *was* the ceiling.
Same symptom — "it feels slow" — but two completely different causes. Also, the
old drive uses the older connector, so the new enclosures would not even fit it.
It stays exactly as it is.

## Choosing the adapter, and talking myself down from overspending

Spent a while comparing enclosures. The useful realisation: for a spinning
drive, the expensive one is no faster than the cheap one. The premium only buys
a named chip inside, a better reliability record, and the ability to read a
drive's health through it — none of which changes the actual speed, because the
drive's own platters are the limit long before the connection is. Best case, a
new enclosure roughly doubles the first drive's speed and no more; the tenfold
figure the connector promises only applies to solid-state drives.

Checked one budget option's missing details against the maker's own site and
could not find them — they simply do not publish the chip or the faster-protocol
support. Took that silence as the answer: assume it is absent. A near-identical
cheap one, though, does list both the faster protocol and this system, so it
became the sensible budget pick. The plan: buy cheap, but test it the moment it
arrives — confirm the faster speed and read the whole drive once — and return it
if it misbehaves, rather than paying extra just to skip that check.

One last thing worth writing down, because it is easy to get backwards: a bad
adapter cannot wear out a disk or create bad spots on it — that damage is the
drive's own doing, from age and use. But a flaky adapter *can* still lose data,
by cutting a write off halfway. So the reason not to buy the very cheapest is
not to protect the disk's surface, which is safe either way, but to protect what
is being written to it. And always eject properly before unplugging, for the
same reason.
